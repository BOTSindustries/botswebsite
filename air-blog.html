The Air Pollution Crisis in Nepal: Understanding the Challenges and Path to Solutions

  • Environment, Health
  • 05 Comments

Air pollution in Nepal has reached alarming levels, impacting millions of lives and threatening the country's environmental and public health. Despite its reputation as the land of the Himalayas, the purity of Nepal’s air is becoming a rare commodity.

The Scale of the Problem

Nepal has been ranked among the most polluted countries in the world, with cities like Kathmandu frequently registering hazardous levels of pollution. PM2.5 and PM10 are the major pollutants, far exceeding the World Health Organization's (WHO) recommended limits. For example, daily averages often surpass 100 µg/m³ during winter months in Kathmandu.

Sources of Air Pollution

Transportation: Vehicle emissions from the increasing number of cars, buses, and motorbikes contribute significantly to air pollution.

Industrial Activities: Factories, brick kilns, and cement plants in the Terai region are major sources of pollution.

Burning of Biomass: In rural areas, burning wood and other biomass for cooking leads to substantial pollution.

Transboundary Pollution: During winter, winds carry pollutants from neighboring India, exacerbating air quality issues in Nepal.

Health Impacts

The health impacts of air pollution are severe, especially for vulnerable populations. Respiratory diseases, cardiovascular issues, and premature death are rising due to long-term exposure to PM2.5 and PM10. A 2019 report estimated that air pollution contributed to over 42,000 premature deaths in Nepal.

Environmental Impact

Air pollution is not only affecting human health but also damaging Nepal's natural environment. Pollutants settle on the soil and water bodies, impacting agriculture and biodiversity. The deposition of black carbon on the Himalayas accelerates glacier melting, further contributing to climate change.

Government Initiatives and Public Response

The government of Nepal has made efforts to mitigate air pollution through the National Air Quality Standard (NAQS) and the installation of monitoring stations. However, enforcement is a challenge. Public awareness is growing, and organizations like VayuDrishti+ are playing a crucial role in educating citizens about air pollution through workshops and local initiatives.

Path to Solutions

Stricter Enforcement: Implementing more rigorous emission standards on industries and vehicles is crucial.

Clean Energy Alternatives: Reducing the use of biomass for cooking through clean energy solutions like solar and biogas can lower household pollution.

Improving Public Transportation: Promoting electric vehicles (EVs) and enhancing public transport can reduce vehicle emissions.

Transboundary Cooperation: Nepal must collaborate with neighboring countries to tackle cross-border pollution.

Public Engagement: Educating and empowering citizens to make informed choices about air quality can create a culture of responsibility toward cleaner air.

Conclusion

Nepal's air pollution crisis is a pressing concern. The path forward requires collaboration between the government, public, and international partners. Prioritizing stricter standards, clean energy, and improved transportation systems will help Nepal breathe cleaner air once again.
